# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

The primary audience is recruiters, hiring managers, engineering leads, and product leads evaluating Alex Guseynov for frontend engineering roles. They usually arrive with limited time and need to assess technical seniority, implementation quality, product judgment, and whether Alex is likely to strengthen a real frontend team rather than only ship isolated screens.

Their job is to decide whether Alex is worth a serious hiring conversation. The portfolio should make that decision easier by presenting a clear frontend profile: React, TypeScript, Vue, UI systems, modernization, maintainability, delivery reliability, and practical AI-assisted execution that still reads as disciplined engineering rather than novelty.

## Product Purpose

This site is a personal portfolio and hiring surface for Alex Guseynov, a senior frontend engineer with 10 years of production interface experience. It exists to turn professional credibility into direct outreach: email conversations, CV downloads, GitHub review, and LinkedIn follow-up.

Success means the visitor leaves with confidence that Alex can build polished product surfaces, improve frontend systems, modernize legacy interfaces responsibly, and use modern AI tools to accelerate implementation without lowering the engineering bar.

## Brand Personality

Precise, senior, deliberate.

The tone should feel direct, technically credible, and quietly confident. It should communicate craft through structure, language, and execution quality, not through inflated claims or decorative theatrics. The portfolio should feel like it was built by someone who cares about implementation details because those details affect product quality, team velocity, and long-term maintainability.

## Anti-references

Avoid generic template portfolios that feel interchangeable across developers. Avoid overdone SaaS landing page conventions, especially inflated claims, decorative metric blocks, and empty conversion language. Avoid playful junior developer aesthetics that make the work feel less serious than it is. Avoid corporate resume pages that reduce the site to a static CV. Avoid AI-generated dark glass UI patterns where glow, blur, and cards substitute for judgment.

## Design Principles

Show credibility through specificity: prefer concrete work, technologies, outcomes, and operating contexts over broad claims.

Make quality visible: the interface should demonstrate the same attention to structure, polish, responsive behavior, and interaction detail that the copy promises.

Treat AI as leverage, not identity: present AI-powered capability as a force multiplier inside strong frontend execution, not as a replacement for engineering judgment.

Reserve the first screen for a memorable interaction: the opening viewport should be able to host a future interactive canvas or game-like hero with sound, then hand off cleanly into the evaluative portfolio narrative below.

Keep the path to contact obvious: every section can deepen the story, but the visitor should never have to hunt for email, CV, GitHub, or LinkedIn.

Represent senior frontend work as systems work: emphasize maintainability, component consistency, modernization, testing, and team delivery alongside visual polish.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Aim for WCAG AA as the baseline. Maintain keyboard access, visible focus states, readable contrast, and controls that do not depend on color alone. Respect reduced-motion preferences, especially for animation, sound-triggered interactions, and any future canvas hero behavior. Keep copy clear for non-native English readers and avoid jargon that does not help a hiring decision. Any future sound in the hero must be optional, interruptible, and never required to understand or navigate the page.
